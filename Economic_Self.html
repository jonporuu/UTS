Economic Self

Overview

Economics is defined as the study of things that a person is lacking, how they make use of the things they have and even making the right decision. This is also regards to the material prosperity of an individual. In material aspects, it refers to how an individual’s possession, status and economic decision shape themselves in both self-concept and identity. The term "economic self" refers to an individual's understanding and relationship with their economic resources, including their ability to meet basic needs, make purchasing decisions, and how they define themselves through material possessions and financial standing. It encompasses one's financial well-being, purchasing behaviors, and the role of material possessions in shaping identity and self-concept. It refers to how individuals perceive and manage their financial resources, decisions, and roles in the economy. It includes one’s attitudes toward money, spending habits, budgeting, saving, and career-related financial goals. Understanding one’s economic self is part of developing personal responsibility and autonomy, which is crucial in transitioning from dependence to financial independence (Diener & Biswas-Diener, 2002).

1. Material Possessions and Identity

Material possessions, such as clothing, home, and other belongings, can be seen as extensions of the self, contributing to one's self-image and identity. People often choose to acquire goods that align with their personality and values, using them to express their identity and social status. The pursuit of acquiring goods and filling homes with objects can be a way for individuals and families to express their character and sense of self, especially in consumer-driven societies.

2. Economic Well-being and Self-Concept

An individual's financial situation, including income, expenses, and debt, significantly impacts their economic self. Financial constraints and the availability of resources also influence purchasing decisions and how people perceive their economic standing. Material possessions and financial status can affect how individuals view themselves and how they are perceived by others.

3. Economic Self as a Concept

  • The concept of the economic self is related to consumer psychology, which explores the cognitive processes behind consumer choices and the influence of marketing and social factors on purchasing behavior.
  • It also relates to economic consciousness, which refers to the socialization and professionalization of the individual as it relates to economic activity.
  • Economic self-determination, or the capacity to determine the conditions of labor, production, acquisition, and distribution, is another aspect of the economic self.

4. Factors Influencing the Economic Self

  • Financial constraints: Limited resources can impact purchasing decisions and overall well-being.
  • Availability of goods and services: The accessibility of desired items influences consumption patterns and economic choices.
  • Social influence: The opinions and preferences of friends and family can affect purchasing behaviors and financial decisions.
  • Professional development and self-determination: Career choices and the ability to make independent decisions about one's work and finances also contribute to the economic self.

Economic Capacity and Self-Sufficiency

An individual's capacity to meet their basic needs through income and make purchasing decisions based on personal and social factors. It encompasses aspects like income, expenses, debt, employment status, and education, as well as the ability to achieve economic self-sufficiency.

Self-sufficiency is the ability of individuals and families to consistently meet their needs, both physically and emotionally. It is about building a strong sense of inner security and independence, allowing one to handle life’s challenges with confidence and resilience.

1. Economic Self-Sufficiency

This is the ability to meet one's basic needs with minimal external assistance. It involves having sufficient economic resources to cover necessities like food, shelter, and healthcare.

2. Material Self

This aspect of the economic self includes possessions and their connection to one's identity. It can encompass things like clothing, home, family, and even the body itself.

3. Factors Influencing the Economic Self

  • Income: The amount of money an individual earns is a primary determinant of their economic well-being.
  • Expenses: Managing expenses effectively is crucial for maintaining financial stability.
  • Debt: High levels of debt can negatively impact the economic self, limiting spending and future financial opportunities.
  • Employment Status: Job security and career prospects significantly influence an individual's economic standing.
  • Education: Higher levels of education often correlate with increased earning potential and economic opportunities.

4. Consumer Psychology

This field studies the cognitive processes behind consumer choices and how marketing and other external factors influence purchasing behavior. Understanding the economic self requires consideration of these psychological aspects.

5. Economic Ideology

Economic ideologies shape how people view the economy and its role in society. They influence perspectives on issues like economic growth, income distribution, and the role of government in the economy.

6. Economic Freedom

This refers to the extent to which individuals have the freedom to make economic choices, including production, trade, and consumption.

Economic Consciousness

Economic consciousness is the result of socialization and professionalization of the subject that acquires a particular significance in human affairs. It refers to an individual’s awareness and understanding when it comes to economic systems, relationship with material possessions, and factors shaping their identity and behavior based on their possessions. This is also closely tied to the concept of the “material self,” which is defined as possessions or economic standing.

John Heskett, a British writer and lecturer on the economic, political, cultural, and human value of industrial design and a professor at the Institute of Design at Illinois Institute of Technology, said that design combines the “need” and “desire” in the form of a practical object that can also reflect an individual's identity and aspirations through its form and decoration.

Semiotic Analysis

Semiotic analysis is the study of signs, introduced by Roland Barthes. It examines how words, paragraphs, images, and objects can work as a language to communicate a range of ideas, associations, and feelings.

  • Signifier: Refers to the physical form.
  • Signified: Refers to the mental concept.

Theory of the Meaning of Material Possessions (Dittmar, 1992, 2004)

Functions:

  • Instrumental functions: How an object or possession is used to achieve practical or functional needs.
  • Social symbolic functions: How a material possession is used to communicate personal qualities, social standing, group affiliation, and gender roles.
  • Categorical functions: How a material possession is used to define group membership or social status.
  • Self-expressive functions: How an individual communicates their thoughts, feelings, and individuality to others or themselves.

Symbolic Concepts:

  • Symbolic Motives: The ways individuals express themselves using possessions or behaviors.
  • Symbolic Self-Completion: The theory that individuals strive to acquire and display symbols associated with their ideal self.
  • Symbolic Interactionism: Emphasizes how an individual develops a sense of self through social interactions.
  • Material Self (William James): Includes possessions, places, and even people that an individual considers as an extension of their identity.

Functions of Material Possessions:

  • Instrumental
  • Social/Symbolic
  • Categorical
  • Self-expressive

Consumer Behavior

Consumer behavior refers to making decisions based on both practical needs and the symbolic meaning of products.

Importance of Economic Self

1. Promotes Independence

Having economic self means not having to rely heavily on others—like parents, government aid, or loans—to meet basic needs. This independence gives a person freedom to make their own choices, live on their own terms, and face emergencies or challenges with greater confidence.

Example: A young adult who learns to budget, save, and earn their own income doesn’t need to ask their parents for every expense.

2. Builds Financial Responsibility

When someone is in charge of their own money, they naturally develop habits like budgeting, saving, and investing. These habits build long-term wealth and help avoid debt traps.

Good economic self = smarter money choices = better future.

3. Prepares for Future Goals

People who develop their economic self early are more prepared to achieve goals like going to college, starting a business, buying a house, or even supporting a family. It lays the foundation for dreams to become reality.

Without economic stability, even the best plans can fall apart.

4. Helps in National Development

On a bigger scale, citizens who are economically self-sufficient contribute more to society. They pay taxes, support local businesses, and don’t overly depend on public funds—helping the economy grow.

A financially stable population = a stronger nation.

5. Encourages Self-Worth and Confidence

There’s a strong link between financial health and mental health. People who are in control of their economic situation often feel more confident, capable, and less anxious.

Being able to provide for yourself feels good. That’s economic dignity.

Conclusion

A well-developed economic self is vital in achieving long-term financial security and stability. It helps individuals make informed decisions about budgeting, saving, investing, and career planning. Moreover, it contributes to one’s overall sense of control and self-efficacy, particularly in adulthood, where financial responsibilities significantly impact quality of life (Lusardi & Mitchell, 2014).

Understanding the economic self is crucial in the modern world where economic decision-making is a daily activity. It affects not only personal financial stability but also overall well-being and life satisfaction. By recognizing the importance of responsible money management, individuals can reduce financial stress, build wealth, and contribute meaningfully to society (OECD, 2020).

Tips/Strategies for Developing Economic Self

1. Practice Self-Awareness with Money

What it means: Understand your money habits and what triggers them.

How to do it:

  • Record your spending (even ₱20 snacks).
  • Ask yourself why you made each purchase — need, habit, boredom?

2. Distinguish Needs vs Wants

What it means: Learn to prioritize survival and essential items over luxuries.

How to do it:

  • Make a list of "Needs" and "Wants" before buying.
  • Apply the 80/20 rule — 80% on needs/savings, 20% on wants.

3. Practice Delayed Gratification

What it means: Learn to wait before spending, especially on non-essentials.

How to do it:

  • Try the 30-Day Rule: wait 30 days before buying a want.
  • Create a “wish list” instead of buying right away.

4. Avoid Comparing Yourself to Others

What it means: Don’t tie your worth to what others have or show online.

How to do it:

  • Limit social media that makes you feel insecure.
  • Focus on what makes you valuable — not your stuff.

5. Set Financial Goals (Short & Long Term)

What it means: Make a plan for what you want to achieve financially.

How to do it:

  • Use SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound).
  • Example goals: “Save ₱500 in 1 month,” or “Buy a secondhand laptop by November.”

6. Educate Yourself About Basic Finance

What it means: Learn the basics of handling money.

How to do it:

  • Watch YouTube videos, read finance blogs, or talk to parents/mentors.
  • Practice budgeting with your weekly allowance.

7. Define Your Worth Beyond Money

What it means: You are more than your clothes, gadgets, or cash.

How to do it:

  • Reflect on your values, skills, and relationships.
  • Ask: “If I had no money, what would still make me valuable?”

Challenges and Solutions in Developing Economic Self

Some common challenges in developing a strong economic self include lack of financial literacy, peer pressure, impulsive spending, and limited access to income sources. Young adults, especially students, often struggle to balance personal wants with long-term financial goals. The influence of social media can also distort perceptions of wealth and success, making responsible financial behavior more difficult (Shim et al., 2010).

Suggested Starting Points

  • Start with budgeting: Track income and expenses to understand spending patterns.
  • Set financial goals: Have short-term and long-term goals like emergency savings or investing.
  • Improve financial literacy: Take courses or use online tools to understand savings, credit, and investing.
  • Avoid debt traps: Be cautious with credit cards and loans.
  • Reflect regularly: Monitor progress and adjust habits as needed.

Key Challenges

  • Financial misinformation
  • Limited education in personal finance
  • Economic inequality
  • Psychological biases like instant gratification
  • Difficulty managing part-time income
  • Struggles developing savings habits amidst increasing expenses (Mandell, 2008)

Practical Tips

  • Practice mindful spending: Distinguish between needs and wants.
  • Build emergency savings: Save a portion of any income or allowance regularly.
  • Educate yourself: Read books or attend seminars on personal finance.
  • Use budgeting tools: Apps like Mint or spreadsheets can help manage finances.
  • Set realistic goals: Start with small, achievable financial targets.
